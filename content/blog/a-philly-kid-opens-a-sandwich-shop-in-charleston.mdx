I grew up in Northeast Philadelphia, in the kind of neighborhood where Sunday dinner wasn't optional.

My grandmother made gravy — that's red sauce, for anyone not from South Philly — every single week. My grandfather had opinions about bread. Loud, specific, non-negotiable opinions about bread. The corner deli on our block was a place of genuine community, where people knew your name and your order before you opened your mouth.

That world shaped everything I cook.

## The Long Way Around

I went to the Culinary Institute of America in Hyde Park, New York — one of the best culinary schools in the country. I graduated, went back to Philadelphia, and spent years working in kitchens that had nothing to do with sandwiches.

Fine dining. White tablecloths. Tasting menus. The whole serious-chef pipeline.

I moved to Birmingham, Alabama in 2003 and opened AMBA as Executive Chef and Partner. Four stars from The Birmingham News. Then I opened **(local)** restaurant and cocktail bar in 2005 — four stars again, "Best New Restaurant," "Top Three Chefs," a dinner at the James Beard Foundation.

**(local)** was featured in Bon Appétit. The New York Times. Cooking with Paula Deen. For a kid from Northeast Philly, that was surreal.

## The TV Show

In January 2015, I competed on CNBC's *Restaurant Startup* — a show hosted by Joe Bastianich and Tim Love where chefs pitch restaurant concepts for real investment.

I won.

My concept was a crudo and sous-vide focused modern American restaurant. My customer food survey was 100% — the first perfect score in the show's history. Both hosts offered me deals.

I'm telling you this not to brag, but because it's important context for what came next. At the peak of my career credibility, when I could have opened a serious fine dining concept with investors and press and all of it —

I wanted to make sandwiches.

## Why Sandwiches

Here's the thing nobody tells you about fine dining: after years of plating microscopic portions and building 14-component dishes, what you actually crave is something you can hold in your hands.

The food I loved most — the food that actually moved me — was the food from my childhood. The corner deli. My grandmother's kitchen. The Italian hoagie from the place two blocks from where I grew up that I have spent 30 years trying to recreate and improve.

Sandwiches, done right, are one of the most technically demanding things you can make. The balance of textures, the acid, the fat, the salt, the bread — every element has to work. There is nowhere to hide on a sandwich. You taste everything.

I wanted to make sandwiches that tasted like they were made by someone who had spent 20 years learning how not to make bad food.

## Falling in Love with Charleston

I moved to Charleston in January 2021, in the middle of a pandemic, as a single parent to my son Steven. I needed a fresh start in a city that was alive and growing and had a food culture I could contribute to.

I found a 700 square foot space at 207A Saint Philip Street — the corner of Spring and Saint Philip, in the Cannonborough-Elliotborough neighborhood. It had been a poke bowl restaurant. It was tiny. It was imperfect. It was exactly right.

I opened The Pass in the summer of 2021.

## The "Such a Nice Italian Boy"

The first sandwich I put on the menu was the one I had been building in my head for years: mortadella with pistachio, hot and sweet soppressata, chili relish, burrata, sharp provolone, red onion, greens, tomato, housemade Italian vinaigrette on ciabatta.

I called it "Such a Nice Italian Boy."

It went viral on Instagram before I even knew what that meant for a restaurant. People were driving across the city for it. Then driving from other cities. Then flying in and making it their first stop.

I still make it the same way I made it on day one.

## What The Pass Is Now

Four years later, we have a second location in Mount Pleasant. We have The Italian Boy After Dark — an intimate six-course tasting experience that happens inside the same shop after we close the deli counter. We have been in Southern Living, Delish, Food Republic, the Post & Courier, Charleston Magazine, and Bravo's *Southern Charm*.

A Maserati drove through our wall and we came back stronger.

But here is what The Pass actually is, at its core: it's the corner deli from my childhood, rebuilt with everything I learned in 20 years of cooking. It is the place where Anthony's grandmother's gravy lives alongside a James Beard-level technique. It is unpretentious and serious at the same time.

It is, as we like to say, **unapologetically Italian and deceptively good.**

Come in. Get a sandwich. Tell us what you think.

*— Anthony Marini*
