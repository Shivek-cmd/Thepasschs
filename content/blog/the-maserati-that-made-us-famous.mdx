My phone rang at 3:30AM.

I didn't recognize the number, so I almost didn't pick up. I'm glad I did.

"Mr. Marini? This is Charleston Police. There's been an incident at your restaurant."

That was how Valentine's Day weekend 2024 began for me.

## What Happened

At 2:09AM on February 13, 2024, a driver ran a red light on Spring Street at full speed. She hit a Jeep at the intersection — the kind of impact that flips a car — and the Maserati kept going. Right through the wooden façade of The Pass at 207A Saint Philip Street.

I arrived to find my front wall open to the street. Lumber everywhere. Glass. The front of a luxury Italian sports car sitting where my tables used to be.

The kitchen, miraculously, was untouched.

I walked through the debris for about an hour before I found it: the Maserati trident — the brand's iconic three-pronged hood ornament — sitting in the rubble like it had been placed there on purpose. I picked it up and put it in my pocket.

It hangs on our wall today.

## The Five Days That Followed

We were closed for five days. That's it.

Not five weeks. Not five months. *Five days.*

My team showed up every single day. We didn't lay anyone off. We didn't close the catering arm. We built a service window in the damaged facade and started serving sandwiches through a hole in our wall while contractors worked behind us.

The community's response was something I was not prepared for.

Charleston chefs began signing the boarded-up exterior — starting with a Philadelphia chef who was in town for Charleston Wine & Food. By the time we reopened fully, that board was covered in signatures, drawings, messages of support from locals, tourists, regulars, and people who had never even been to The Pass.

**We served through that window for seven months.**

## The Quote That Went Everywhere

Someone asked me how I felt about the car being a Maserati — a *Italian* car, no less, crashing into an Italian deli.

I said: *"I'm glad it was an Italian car. If it was a German car, I'd be pissed."*

That got around.

## What I Learned

You don't know what your business really is until something tries to end it.

The Pass was never just a sandwich shop. It was — and is — a community gathering place, a place where people feel seen and fed and taken care of. The night of the crash proved that. Within hours, people were calling to check on us, offering to help, showing up at the boarded-up window just to say they were thinking of us.

We fully reopened in September 2024. The interior looks better than it ever has. And yes — the Maserati trident still hangs on the wall. We earned that trophy.

> "A setback is a setup for a comeback. This city showed up for us. Now we show up for this city — every day."

If you haven't been to The Pass since the reopening, come see what we did with the place. And if you were one of the people who showed up during those seven months, thank you. You are the reason we are still here.

*— Anthony Marini, Chef & Owner*
