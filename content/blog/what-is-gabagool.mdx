At least once a day, someone walks up to our counter, points at "The Italian Girl" on the menu, and asks:

*"What's... Gabagool?"*

If you grew up watching The Sopranos, you've heard it. If you grew up in a South Philly rowhome — like I did — you've eaten it your whole life. If you're neither of those things, allow me to introduce you to one of the finest cured meats Italy has ever produced.

## Gabagool Is Capicola

That's the short answer. **Gabagool** is the Italian-American pronunciation of **Capicola** (also written *capocollo* or *coppa*), a cured pork product made from the neck and shoulder of the pig.

The name comes from *capo* (head) and *collo* (neck) — literally "head-neck." The New York and New Jersey Italian-American communities transformed the pronunciation over generations, the way languages evolve when they cross an ocean and settle into a neighborhood.

Capicola → Capagool → Gabagool.

That's Philadelphia and New York Italian-American for you. We turn everything into something more efficient and more colorful.

## What Does It Taste Like?

Gabagool is **deeply savory, lightly spiced, and silky in a way that most cured meats are not.**

Unlike prosciutto, which is dry and delicate, capicola has more fat marbling through it — which means more flavor and a richer mouthfeel. It's typically seasoned with wine, garlic, and spices (sweet paprika, black pepper, sometimes red pepper for the hot version), then dry-cured and aged for at least 60 days.

The result is something that tastes like a combination of prosciutto and salami, but more nuanced than either.

At The Pass, we use **Hot Coppa** (the spicier version) on our TACONY-6931 sandwich and regular **Gabagool** on The Italian Girl. They are not interchangeable — trust the menu.

## Why Do We Use It?

Because it makes sandwiches that people remember.

You can put turkey on a sandwich. You can put ham on a sandwich. But when you build a sandwich with gabagool and burrata and a housemade cherry pepper aioli — you've made something that people come back for.

That's the whole philosophy behind The Pass. We take an ingredient with 500 years of Italian history, pair it with something local and fresh from Charleston, and let the two have a conversation on a piece of toasted ciabatta.

## How to Pronounce It (If You Want to Impress Someone)

The authentic Italian pronunciation is *kah-poh-KOH-lah*.

But if you're at our counter, feel free to call it Gabagool. We'll know exactly what you mean — and we'll be silently impressed that you know the word at all.
